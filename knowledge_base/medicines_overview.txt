# HEALTH INFORMATION REFERENCE — MEDICINES OVERVIEW
# Source type: General Medical Reference (Educational Use Only)
# CRITICAL NOTICE: This document is for identification and educational purposes ONLY.
# DO NOT use this information to self-prescribe, alter dosages, or start/stop any medication.
# Always consult a licensed pharmacist or medical doctor before taking any medicine.

=== EXPIRY DATE — UNIVERSAL SAFETY RULE ===

ALWAYS check the expiry date printed on the medicine packaging before use.
- Expired medicines may have reduced effectiveness or could cause harm.
- Do NOT use medicines with removed, illegible, or altered packaging.
- If unsure about a medicine's identity, safety, or expiry — consult a pharmacist immediately.
- Store all medicines as per packaging instructions (temperature, humidity, light exposure).
- Keep medicines out of reach of children.
- Never share prescription medicines with others.

=== ANALGESICS AND ANTIPYRETICS (PAIN RELIEF / FEVER REDUCERS) ===

Medicine: Paracetamol (Acetaminophen)
Common Brand Names: Crocin, Tylenol, Dolo, Panadol, Calpol
Common Use: Relief of mild to moderate pain (headache, toothache, body ache), reduction of fever
Tablet Appearance (common): White, round or oblong tablet; imprints vary by brand (e.g., "P500", "TYLENOL")
Safety Notes:
- Do not exceed the recommended daily dose (dosage varies — follow package label)
- Avoid alcohol while taking this medicine
- Use with caution in liver disease patients
- Consult doctor for use in children — weight-based dosing required
Expiry Warning: Check packaging; do not use expired paracetamol

Medicine: Ibuprofen
Common Brand Names: Brufen, Advil, Motrin, Nurofen
Common Use: Pain relief (headache, menstrual pain, dental pain, muscle aches), anti-inflammatory, fever reduction
Tablet Appearance (common): Coated tablet; typically orange, red, or brown; various imprints
Safety Notes:
- Take with food to reduce stomach irritation
- Avoid in kidney disease, stomach ulcers, or during pregnancy (especially third trimester)
- Not recommended for long-term use without medical supervision
- Avoid in patients taking blood thinners
Expiry Warning: Check packaging before use

Medicine: Aspirin (Acetylsalicylic Acid)
Common Brand Names: Disprin, Bayer Aspirin, Ecosprin
Common Use: Pain relief, fever, anti-inflammatory; low-dose aspirin used for cardiovascular conditions (only under doctor supervision)
Tablet Appearance (common): White round tablet; often with cross score mark; "ASPIRIN" or brand imprint
Safety Notes:
- Do NOT give aspirin to children under 16 (risk of Reye's syndrome)
- Avoid if allergic to NSAIDs, history of stomach ulcers, or on blood thinners
- Cardiovascular use (low-dose) must only be started/stopped on doctor's advice
Expiry Warning: Check packaging; expired aspirin may develop vinegar smell — discard

Medicine: Diclofenac
Common Brand Names: Voveran, Voltaren, Cataflam
Common Use: Pain and inflammation relief for muscle aches, arthritis, sprains
Safety Notes:
- Take with food
- Not for long-term use without doctor oversight
- Avoid in kidney/heart/liver disease
- Topical gel form available for joint/muscle pain (applied to skin)
Expiry Warning: Check packaging before use

=== ANTACIDS AND GI MEDICINES ===

Medicine: Antacids (Calcium carbonate, Magnesium hydroxide, Aluminum hydroxide combinations)
Common Brand Names: Gelusil, Digene, Tums, Maalox, Eno (powder form)
Common Use: Neutralizes stomach acid; relief from heartburn, acid indigestion, upset stomach
Tablet Appearance: Chewable tablets, often white or mint-colored; powder or liquid forms also common
Safety Notes:
- Short-term use for symptomatic relief only
- Do not use as substitute for prescribed acid-reducing medications
- High intake may interfere with absorption of other medicines
- Consult doctor if heartburn is frequent (more than 2× per week)
Expiry Warning: Check packaging; powders especially should be within expiry

Medicine: Omeprazole / Pantoprazole (Proton Pump Inhibitors)
Common Brand Names: Omez, Pan 40, Prilosec, Nexium
Common Use: Reduces stomach acid production; used for acid reflux, ulcers (under medical supervision)
Safety Notes:
- Typically a prescription medication in many countries
- Long-term use should only be under doctor supervision
- Do not start or stop PPI therapy without consulting a doctor
Expiry Warning: Check packaging

Medicine: Ondansetron / Domperidone (Anti-emetics)
Common Brand Names: Zofran (ondansetron), Domstal, Motilium (domperidone)
Common Use: Relief of nausea and vomiting
Safety Notes:
- Use under doctor's guidance especially in children
- Cardiac patients should consult doctor before use of domperidone
Expiry Warning: Check packaging

Medicine: Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS)
Common Brand Names: Electral, WHO-ORS, Pedialyte
Common Use: Rehydration in cases of diarrhea, vomiting, dehydration
Form: Powder sachet dissolved in water; liquid; tablets
Safety Notes:
- Safe for all ages when used as directed
- Critical for children and elderly with diarrhea/vomiting
- Prepare fresh solution — do not store prepared ORS for more than 24 hours
- Seek medical attention if diarrhea persists beyond 48–72 hours
Expiry Warning: Check sachet packaging carefully

=== ANTIHISTAMINES (ALLERGY MEDICINES) ===

Medicine: Cetirizine
Common Brand Names: Zyrtec, Alerid, Okacet, Cetzine
Common Use: Relief of allergy symptoms — sneezing, runny nose, itchy eyes, skin rashes, hives
Tablet Appearance: White or light-colored tablet, oblong or round; "ZYRTEC" or similar imprint
Safety Notes:
- May cause drowsiness in some individuals
- Avoid alcohol
- Use with caution when operating machinery
- Second-generation antihistamine — generally less sedating than older types
Expiry Warning: Check tablet blister pack or bottle

Medicine: Loratadine
Common Brand Names: Claritin, Lorfast, Lorid
Common Use: Non-drowsy allergy relief — hay fever, allergic rhinitis, skin allergy
Safety Notes:
- Less sedating than older antihistamines
- Check package for use in children (age/weight restrictions)
Expiry Warning: Check packaging

Medicine: Chlorpheniramine (older antihistamine)
Common Brand Names: Piriton, Chlor-Trimeton
Common Use: Allergy relief, cold symptoms
Safety Notes:
- Causes significant drowsiness — do not drive or operate machinery
- Prefer newer antihistamines unless specifically directed by a doctor
Expiry Warning: Check packaging

=== ANTIBIOTICS — IMPORTANT NOTICE ===

Antibiotics are PRESCRIPTION medicines. They must only be taken under a doctor's prescription.
- Do NOT self-prescribe antibiotics.
- Do NOT share antibiotics with others.
- Complete the full course even if feeling better (unless instructed otherwise by a doctor).
- Do NOT stop antibiotics early.
- Antibiotic resistance is a serious public health concern — misuse contributes to resistant infections.

Common antibiotic classes (reference only — NOT for self-prescription):
- Amoxicillin: Bacterial infections (respiratory, urinary, dental)
- Azithromycin: Respiratory infections, some skin infections
- Ciprofloxacin: Urinary tract infections, some gastrointestinal infections
- Metronidazole: Anaerobic bacterial infections, certain gut infections
Note: These are listed for IDENTIFICATION purposes only. All antibiotic use requires a DOCTOR's prescription.

=== COUGH AND COLD MEDICINES ===

Medicine: Dextromethorphan (Cough Suppressant)
Common Brand Names: Benylin, Delsym, Robitussin DM
Common Use: Suppresses dry, non-productive cough
Safety Notes:
- Not for use in children under 4 years
- Not recommended for cough with excess mucus
- Do not combine with other cold medicines containing the same ingredient (risk of overdose)
Expiry Warning: Check bottle or blister pack

Medicine: Guaifenesin (Expectorant)
Common Brand Names: Mucinex, Grilinctus
Common Use: Loosens and thins chest mucus, makes cough more productive
Safety Notes:
- Drink plenty of fluids while using expectorants
- Not for dry cough
Expiry Warning: Check packaging

Combination Cold Medicines:
Many OTC cold tablets are combinations (antihistamine + decongestant + analgesic). Always read the label.
Do not double-dose ingredients if taking multiple cold medicines simultaneously.
Examples: Sinarest, D-Cold Total, Nyquil, Dayquil

=== VITAMINS AND SUPPLEMENTS ===

Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid):
Common Use: Immune support, antioxidant; may help reduce duration of cold symptoms
Safety Notes: Generally safe; high doses may cause digestive upset
Expiry Warning: Check packaging

Vitamin D:
Common Use: Bone health, immune function
Safety Notes: High-dose supplementation should be under doctor's guidance (risk of toxicity at very high doses)
Expiry Warning: Check packaging

Zinc:
Common Use: Immune support, wound healing
Safety Notes: Short-term supplementation during cold may be beneficial; long-term high-dose not recommended without medical advice
Expiry Warning: Check packaging

Iron Supplements:
Common Brand Names: Ferrous sulfate, Ferograd, Hemobion
Common Use: Iron deficiency anemia (must be confirmed by blood test)
Safety Notes:
- May cause constipation and dark stools (normal)
- Take on empty stomach or with Vitamin C for better absorption (unless stomach upset)
- Keep out of reach of children — iron overdose is dangerous in children
Expiry Warning: Check packaging

=== TOPICAL MEDICINES ===

Antiseptic Creams/Solutions:
Examples: Betadine (povidone-iodine), Savlon, Dettol
Common Use: Cleaning minor cuts, scrapes, and wounds
Safety Notes:
- For external use only
- Do not apply to deep wounds or puncture wounds without medical guidance
- Consult doctor for infected wounds

Antihistamine / Hydrocortisone Cream:
Common Use: Relief of localized skin itching and mild rash
Safety Notes:
- Topical steroids (hydrocortisone) should not be used on face, groin, or underarm for prolonged periods without medical advice
- Consult doctor if rash is spreading or infected

=== MEDICINE IMAGE IDENTIFICATION GUIDANCE ===

When analyzing a tablet or medicine image, the following visible features help identification:
1. Imprint code: letters, numbers, or symbols pressed into the tablet
2. Tablet shape: round, oval, oblong, capsule, diamond
3. Tablet color: (note: color can vary by manufacturer)
4. Packaging text: brand name, generic name, strength, manufacturer
5. Blister pack label: usually contains full drug name and dosage

LIMITATION: Tablet appearance alone is not sufficient for reliable identification.
- Many tablets share similar shapes and colors
- Generic versions look different from brand versions
- Imprints must be matched against verified pharmaceutical databases
- If image is blurry, partially visible, or damaged — identification is NOT possible

Always advise the user to:
- Check the packaging label for the full medicine name and instructions
- Consult a pharmacist if they are unsure about any medicine
- Never take an unidentified medicine

=== GENERAL MEDICINE SAFETY PRINCIPLES ===

1. Always read the package insert / information leaflet before taking any medicine.
2. Check: name, strength, indication, dosage instructions, contraindications, and expiry date.
3. Inform your doctor of ALL medicines you are currently taking (including supplements and herbal products) to avoid interactions.
4. Store medicines properly: cool, dry place away from sunlight; some require refrigeration.
5. Dispose of expired or unused medicines safely — do not flush down the drain or discard in regular trash without following local guidelines.
6. Never crush or split tablets unless explicitly advised by a pharmacist (some tablets have special coatings).
7. If you experience unexpected side effects — stop and consult a doctor or pharmacist.
