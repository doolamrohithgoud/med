# HEALTH INFORMATION REFERENCE — GENERAL WELLNESS AND PREVENTIVE HEALTH
# Source type: General Medical Reference (Educational Use Only)
# This document covers general wellness practices and preventive health information.

=== IMPORTANCE OF PREVENTIVE HEALTH ===

Preventive health is the practice of maintaining habits and behaviors that reduce the risk of illness
and promote long-term physical and mental wellbeing.

Key Benefits:
- Reduces risk of chronic disease development
- Enables early detection of health conditions
- Improves quality of life and longevity
- Reduces healthcare burden

=== PHYSICAL ACTIVITY ===

General Guidelines (WHO Recommendations):
- Adults: at least 150–300 minutes of moderate-intensity or 75–150 minutes of vigorous aerobic activity per week
- Children (5–17 years): at least 60 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous activity daily
- Older adults: include balance and strength training exercises to prevent falls

Types of Beneficial Activity:
- Aerobic: walking, jogging, swimming, cycling, dancing (improves cardiovascular health)
- Strength: resistance exercises (supports muscle mass, bone density, metabolism)
- Flexibility: stretching, yoga (improves mobility, reduces injury risk)
- Balance: tai chi, balance exercises (especially important for elderly)

Starting Physical Activity Safely:
- Begin gradually — even 10 minutes of walking daily is beneficial
- Increase intensity and duration progressively
- Stay hydrated before, during, and after exercise
- Warm up and cool down with gentle movement and stretches
- Listen to your body — stop if you feel chest pain, dizziness, or severe discomfort
- If you have existing medical conditions — consult a doctor before starting a new exercise program

Sedentary Behavior:
- Prolonged sitting is associated with increased health risks even in active individuals
- Take short breaks (2–5 minutes of movement) every 30–60 minutes of sitting
- Stand or walk during phone calls

=== SLEEP HEALTH ===

Sleep Recommendations:
- Adults: 7–9 hours per night
- Teenagers: 8–10 hours
- School-age children: 9–11 hours
- Infants and toddlers: More hours with naps (consult pediatric guidelines)

Benefits of Good Sleep:
- Supports immune function
- Promotes mental health and cognitive performance
- Regulates appetite hormones
- Supports cardiovascular health
- Critical for tissue repair and recovery

Common Sleep Disruptors and Solutions:
- Irregular schedule: Aim for consistent sleep and wake times (even on weekends)
- Screens before bed: Avoid screens 30–60 minutes before sleep; blue light suppresses melatonin
- Caffeine: Avoid caffeine after 2 PM (half-life is approximately 5–6 hours)
- Heavy meals: Avoid large meals within 2–3 hours of sleep
- Stress: Wind-down routine (reading, gentle stretching, breathing exercises) helps
- Room environment: Cool, dark, and quiet promotes deeper sleep

When to Talk to a Doctor About Sleep:
- Persistent difficulty falling or staying asleep (>3 weeks)
- Excessive daytime sleepiness despite adequate night sleep
- Loud snoring with gasping (possible sleep apnea — requires medical evaluation)
- Restless or uncomfortable sensations in legs at night

=== NUTRITION AND DIET ===

The Role of Balanced Nutrition:
Good nutrition provides the body with essential macronutrients (carbohydrates, proteins, fats) and
micronutrients (vitamins and minerals) required for all physiological functions.

Key Nutritional Principles:
1. Variety: Eat a wide variety of whole, minimally processed foods
2. Balance: Every meal should ideally include a source of carbohydrate, protein, and healthy fat
3. Moderation: No food group needs to be completely eliminated; moderation is key
4. Timing: Regular meals and avoiding long fasting periods supports stable energy and metabolism

Macronutrient Roles:
- Carbohydrates: Primary energy source; prefer complex carbs (whole grains, legumes, vegetables) over refined sugars
- Proteins: Building block for muscles, immune cells, enzymes; sources include eggs, legumes, dairy, lean meat, fish
- Fats: Essential for brain function, hormone production, vitamin absorption; prefer unsaturated fats (nuts, seeds, olive oil)

Important Vitamins and Minerals:
- Vitamin C: Immune support, antioxidant; sources: citrus fruits, bell peppers, tomatoes, broccoli
- Vitamin D: Bone health, immune function; sources: sunlight exposure, fatty fish, fortified foods; supplementation often needed
- Iron: Oxygen transport; sources: red meat, legumes, dark leafy greens; important for women and children
- Calcium: Bone and teeth health; sources: dairy, fortified plant milks, broccoli, sesame seeds
- B12: Nerve function, red blood cell production; found primarily in animal products; vegetarians/vegans should consult doctor about supplementation
- Folate: Cell division, especially critical in pregnancy; sources: leafy greens, legumes, fortified cereals
- Zinc: Immune function, wound healing; sources: meat, shellfish, legumes, seeds, nuts

Foods to Limit:
- Ultra-processed foods: High in additives, sugar, salt, and unhealthy fats; associated with chronic disease
- Added sugars: Linked to obesity, diabetes risk, dental problems; limit sweetened beverages
- Excess sodium (salt): Linked to hypertension; limit processed/packaged food intake
- Trans fats and hydrogenated oils: Associated with cardiovascular disease risk; avoid where possible
- Excess alcohol: Associated with liver disease, cancer risk, cardiovascular effects, and mental health impacts

Special Nutritional Considerations:
- Pregnant women: Increased needs for folate (folic acid supplement strongly recommended), iron, calcium, iodine; avoid alcohol and limit mercury fish; consult doctor/dietitian
- Children: Age-appropriate portions; variety to prevent nutritional deficiencies; limit junk food
- Elderly: Higher calcium and vitamin D needs; adequate protein to prevent muscle loss; stay well hydrated
- People with chronic conditions (diabetes, kidney disease, cardiovascular disease): Require individualized dietary guidance from a doctor or registered dietitian — do not follow generic online advice

=== MENTAL AND EMOTIONAL WELLBEING ===

Mental health is as important as physical health and is closely interconnected.

Foundations of Good Mental Health:
- Social connection: Maintaining meaningful relationships and community support
- Physical activity: Regular exercise has strong evidence for improving mood and reducing anxiety
- Sleep: Adequate, quality sleep is fundamental to emotional regulation
- Purpose and meaning: Engaging in activities that provide a sense of purpose
- Mindfulness and stress management: Breathing exercises, meditation, journaling

Stress Management Techniques:
- Deep breathing (diaphragmatic breathing): Slow, deep breaths activate the parasympathetic nervous system
- Progressive muscle relaxation: Systematically tensing and releasing muscle groups
- Physical activity: One of the most effective natural mood regulators
- Social support: Talking to trusted people about stressors
- Setting realistic boundaries: Work, social, and personal commitments

When to Seek Professional Mental Health Support:
- Persistent sadness or low mood lasting more than 2 weeks
- Anxiety severely impacting daily functioning
- Difficulty performing basic daily activities
- Substance use as a coping mechanism
- Thoughts of self-harm or hopelessness
- Significant changes in appetite, sleep, or behavior

Reducing Stigma:
Mental health conditions are medical conditions. Seeking help from a mental health professional
(psychiatrist, psychologist, counselor, or therapist) is a sign of strength, not weakness.

=== PREVENTIVE HEALTH SCREENINGS ===

Regular health screenings detect conditions before symptoms develop, enabling earlier treatment.
Frequency varies based on age, gender, and individual risk factors — consult your doctor.

Common Screenings (general guidance — individual schedules vary):
- Blood pressure: Regular monitoring for adults from age 18; annually for hypertension risk
- Blood glucose: Screening for diabetes risk, especially in adults with risk factors (obesity, family history)
- Cholesterol (lipid panel): Adults from age 20 onward; frequency based on results and risk factors
- BMI and weight: Regular monitoring as part of metabolic health assessment
- Vision and hearing: Periodic checks, especially as age advances
- Dental: Recommend dental checkup every 6–12 months
- Cancer screenings: Age- and gender-specific (e.g., cervical smear, mammogram, colorectal cancer screening) — follow national guidelines and doctor's advice

Vaccinations:
Staying up to date with adult vaccinations (influenza, tetanus, COVID-19, hepatitis, pneumococcal, etc.)
is an important part of preventive health — consult your doctor for your vaccination schedule.

=== HEALTHY WEIGHT MANAGEMENT ===

Body weight affects many aspects of health including cardiovascular function, joint health, metabolism, and more.

Healthy Approaches:
- Aim for gradual, sustainable changes (0.5–1 kg / 1–2 lbs per week is a safe target)
- Focus on overall eating pattern — not short-term diets
- Regular physical activity is essential for long-term weight management
- Adequate sleep supports healthy appetite hormone regulation
- Manage stress — stress can drive overeating and weight gain

What to Avoid:
- Extreme caloric restriction or "crash diets" — unsustainable and nutritionally harmful
- "Miracle" pills or unverified supplements — no safe, effective substitute for lifestyle change
- Comparing to others — healthy weight is individual and depends on multiple factors

When to Consult a Doctor:
- Significant unintentional weight gain or loss
- Weight affecting function or quality of life
- Seeking medically supervised weight management
- Considering any weight loss medication or intervention

=== SUBSTANCE USE AND HEALTH ===

Tobacco:
- Smoking and tobacco use are leading preventable causes of disease worldwide
- Associated with: cancer (lung, mouth, throat, bladder, pancreas), cardiovascular disease, COPD, stroke
- Quitting at any age significantly reduces risk — consult doctor about cessation support options

Alcohol:
- Excessive alcohol consumption is associated with liver disease, cancer, neurological effects, mental health disorders
- Recommended limits vary by guidelines — minimizing alcohol is safer
- Do not consume alcohol while taking medications unless confirmed safe by a doctor or pharmacist
- Pregnancy: No safe level of alcohol — abstain completely during pregnancy

=== HYGIENE AND INFECTION PREVENTION ===

Handwashing:
- Most effective simple measure to prevent spread of infections
- Wash hands with soap and water for at least 20 seconds:
  - Before eating or preparing food
  - After using the toilet
  - After sneezing, coughing, or blowing nose
  - After touching animals
  - After handling raw food
- When soap unavailable: use alcohol-based hand sanitizer (at least 60% alcohol)

Respiratory Hygiene:
- Cover mouth and nose with a tissue or elbow when coughing or sneezing
- Dispose of used tissues immediately; wash hands after
- Wear a mask in crowded indoor spaces during respiratory illness

Food Safety:
- Store foods at appropriate temperatures
- Wash fresh produce thoroughly
- Avoid cross-contamination between raw and cooked foods
- Cook foods to recommended internal temperatures

=== SUN SAFETY ===

Excessive sun exposure can cause sunburn, accelerate skin aging, and increase skin cancer risk.
Protective Measures:
- Apply broadspectrum sunscreen (SPF 30 or higher) on exposed skin, even on cloudy days
- Reapply every 2 hours or after swimming or sweating
- Seek shade during peak sun hours (10 AM – 4 PM)
- Wear protective clothing, hat, and UV-blocking sunglasses
- Do not use tanning beds

Regular skin checks: Report any new, changing, or unusual moles or skin lesions to a doctor.
