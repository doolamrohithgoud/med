# HEALTH INFORMATION REFERENCE — FIRST AID AND SAFE GUIDANCE
# Source type: General Medical Reference (Educational Use Only)
# These guidelines describe safe, non-invasive supportive care ONLY.
# They do NOT replace professional medical treatment.
# For any serious, worsening, or unclear situation — consult a licensed medical doctor.

=== IMPORTANCE OF HYDRATION ===

Adequate hydration is one of the most universally beneficial and safe measures for most common ailments.

General Hydration Guidelines:
- Adults: Approximately 8–10 glasses (2–2.5 liters) of water per day under normal conditions
- Increase intake during fever, physical activity, hot weather, or illness
- Signs of dehydration: dark yellow urine, dry mouth, dizziness on standing, reduced urination, fatigue
- Safe rehydration fluids: clean water, clear broths, oral rehydration solution (ORS), coconut water (plain)
- Avoid: excessive caffeine, alcohol, and sugary drinks when dehydrated

Hydration in Specific Situations:
- Fever: Drink cool fluids frequently; water, dilute juices, or clear broth
- Vomiting/Diarrhea: Small, frequent sips of ORS; avoid large gulps that trigger vomiting
- Sore throat: Warm water or warm clear liquids may soothe; stay well hydrated
- Headache: Mild dehydration is a common headache trigger; increase fluid intake

=== REST AND RECOVERY ===

Rest is a fundamental component of recovery from illness. The body's immune system functions more effectively during rest and sleep.

General Rest Guidance:
- Ensure 7–9 hours of sleep per night for adults; more during illness
- Avoid strenuous physical activity while sick with fever or acute illness
- Gentle movement (short walks) may be appropriate for mild conditions and can aid recovery
- Avoid returning to full activity too quickly after fever — resume gradually

Mental Rest:
- Reduce screen time and cognitive load during illness recovery
- Stress reduction supports immune function — quiet, calm environment aids healing

=== FEVER MANAGEMENT — SAFE SUPPORTIVE MEASURES ===

Fever is the body's natural immune response. Supportive care guidelines:

Temperature Monitoring:
- Use a calibrated thermometer (digital oral, axillary, or ear thermometer)
- Record temperature and time to track fever pattern
- Normal body temperature: approximately 36.1–37.2°C (97–99°F)
- Low-grade fever: 37.3–38.0°C (99.1–100.4°F)
- Fever: above 38.0°C (100.4°F)
- High fever: above 39.4°C (103°F) — seek medical attention

Supportive Home Care for Mild Fever:
- Stay in a comfortable room temperature environment
- Wear light, breathable clothing
- Keep well hydrated
- Tepid (lukewarm) sponge bath may help bring down body temperature in adults
- Do NOT use ice-cold water or alcohol rubs

When to Seek Help Immediately:
- Fever above 39.4°C (103°F) in adults
- ANY fever in infants under 3 months
- Fever above 38.5°C in children under 2 years
- Fever lasting more than 3 days without other explanation
- Fever with rash, stiff neck, confusion, or difficulty breathing

=== RESPIRATORY ILLNESS — SAFE SUPPORTIVE CARE ===

For mild-to-moderate cold, flu, or upper respiratory tract infection:

Environment:
- Stay in a warm, well-ventilated room
- Avoid sudden temperature changes (going from cold outside to overheated indoors)
- Use a humidifier if air is dry

Throat Relief:
- Warm water gargling with a small amount of salt (half teaspoon in 8oz warm water) — soothing for sore throat
- Warm liquids (tea, clear broth, warm water with honey for adults — NOT for infants under 1 year)
- Avoid smoking and smoky environments

Nasal Congestion:
- Steam inhalation: A bowl of hot water with a towel over the head — relieves nasal congestion (adults only; caution with children — hot water burn risk)
- Saline nasal drops or spray — safe for all ages, helps clear nasal passages
- Keep head slightly elevated while sleeping

Cough:
- Stay hydrated — fluids help thin mucus
- Honey (adults and children over 1 year) may soothe cough
- Avoid exposure to irritants (smoke, dust, strong smells)

When to Seek Help:
- Breathing difficulty or wheezing
- Cough persisting more than 2 weeks
- Blood in sputum
- High fever not resolving
- Symptoms worsening after initial improvement

=== GASTROINTESTINAL ILLNESS — SAFE SUPPORTIVE CARE ===

For nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea (gastroenteritis):

Oral Rehydration:
- This is the MOST IMPORTANT intervention for diarrhea and vomiting
- Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) — commercially available sachets dissolved in water
- If ORS unavailable: WHO simple home solution — 1 liter of clean water + 6 level teaspoons of sugar + 0.5 teaspoon of salt. (This is only a temporary measure; obtain proper ORS as soon as possible)
- Offer small, frequent sips — do not force large amounts if vomiting

Diet During Recovery:
- Start with clear fluids when vomiting, then progress to bland solid foods as tolerated
- Bland foods: plain rice, plain toast, plain crackers, bananas, boiled potatoes
- Avoid: dairy, fatty foods, spicy foods, high-fiber foods until recovery
- Gradually return to normal diet over 24–48 hours as symptoms improve

Hygiene (prevent spread):
- Wash hands thoroughly with soap after using the toilet and before handling food
- Disinfect bathroom surfaces
- Avoid preparing food for others while symptomatic
- Avoid sharing utensils, cups, towels

When to Seek Help:
- Signs of dehydration (no urination, dry mouth, dizziness, sunken eyes)
- Blood in vomit or diarrhea
- Fever above 102°F
- Vomiting for more than 24 hours (less for infants and elderly)
- Diarrhea persisting more than 3 days
- Any infant or young child with persistent diarrhea/vomiting

=== HEADACHE — SAFE SUPPORTIVE CARE ===

For mild tension headache:

Immediate Measures:
- Rest in a quiet, dim room
- Drink water — dehydration is a very common headache trigger
- Remove source of eye strain (screen breaks, ensure adequate lighting)
- Gentle neck and shoulder stretches — relieves tension
- Cool or warm compress on forehead or back of neck (whichever feels more comfortable)
- Reduce stressors if possible

Prevention:
- Maintain regular sleep schedule
- Take regular breaks from screens (20-20-20 rule: every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds)
- Stay hydrated throughout the day
- Avoid skipping meals (hunger headaches are common)
- Identify and reduce known triggers (stress, certain foods, poor posture)

When to Seek Help IMMEDIATELY:
- Sudden, severe onset "thunderclap" headache (worst headache of life)
- Headache with fever, stiff neck, or rash
- Headache with vision changes, confusion, weakness, or numbness
- Headache following a head injury
- New type of headache in someone over 50

=== MINOR CUTS, SCRAPES, AND WOUNDS ===

For minor skin cuts and scrapes:

Immediate Steps:
1. Wash hands thoroughly before treating wound
2. Control bleeding: Apply gentle, steady pressure with a clean cloth for 10–15 minutes; do not lift cloth to check
3. Clean the wound: Rinse gently with clean running water; remove visible dirt particles gently
4. Apply antiseptic: Use antiseptic solution or cream (povidone-iodine, chlorhexidine, or similar)
5. Cover: Apply a clean bandage or sterile dressing
6. Change dressing daily or when soiled

Signs of Infection (when to seek help):
- Increasing redness, warmth, swelling around wound
- Pus or cloudy discharge
- Red streaks spreading from wound
- Wound not healing within expected time
- Fever following wound

When to Seek Help IMMEDIATELY:
- Deep or gaping wounds that may need stitches
- Wound from animal or human bite
- Wound with embedded object
- Puncture wound (especially hands or feet)
- Wound in diabetic patient or immunocompromised individual
- Tetanus vaccination not up to date for dirty wounds

=== BURNS — SAFE FIRST AID ===

For minor superficial burns (redness, mild pain, small area — e.g., touching hot surface):

Immediate Steps:
1. Cool the burn: Run cool (not cold or iced) water over the burned area for 10–20 minutes
2. Do NOT apply ice, butter, toothpaste, or any home remedies — these cause harm
3. Do NOT break blisters
4. Cover loosely with a clean, non-fluffy bandage or dry cloth
5. Keep burn elevated if possible to reduce swelling

When to Seek Help IMMEDIATELY:
- Burns larger than 3 inches (7.5 cm) in diameter
- Burns on face, hands, feet, genitals, or over joints
- Deep burns (white or brown/black discoloration, no pain due to nerve damage)
- Chemical or electrical burns
- Burns in children or elderly
- Burns with signs of infection

=== ALLERGIC REACTIONS — SAFE FIRST AID ===

For mild allergic reactions (sneezing, itchy eyes, localized hives without breathing difficulty):
- Identify and remove the trigger if possible
- Cool compress on itchy skin area
- Avoid scratching
- Stay calm and comfortable

When to Seek Help IMMEDIATELY (Anaphylaxis Warning Signs):
- Swelling of throat, tongue, or lips
- Difficulty breathing, wheezing, or hoarse voice
- Rapid or weak pulse
- Dizziness or loss of consciousness
- Widespread hives with any systemic symptoms
These signs indicate a MEDICAL EMERGENCY — call emergency services immediately

=== SLEEP AND REST HYGIENE ===

Good sleep supports immune function, mental health, and recovery:

Recommendations:
- Adults: 7–9 hours per night
- Consistent sleep and wake times (even on weekends)
- Dark, quiet, cool bedroom environment
- Reduce screen exposure 30–60 minutes before bed
- Avoid caffeine after 2 PM
- Avoid heavy meals close to bedtime
- Gentle activity during the day promotes better night sleep

Sleep Disturbance:
- Short-term sleep problems often relate to stress, illness, or schedule disruption
- Persistent insomnia (>3 weeks) — consult a doctor

=== BALANCED NUTRITION — GENERAL PRINCIPLES ===

A balanced diet supports immune function and recovery:

Key Principles:
- Include a variety of colorful vegetables and fruits daily (vitamins, minerals, fiber)
- Include adequate protein (legumes, eggs, dairy, lean meats, fish — per dietary preferences)
- Whole grains preferred over refined grains (sustained energy, fiber)
- Healthy fats: nuts, seeds, olive oil (in moderation)
- Limit: ultra-processed foods, excess sugar, excess salt, fried foods
- Stay hydrated with water as the primary beverage

During Illness:
- Appetite reduction is common during fever or illness — do not force eating
- Prioritize fluids
- Small, frequent soft or bland meals as tolerated
- Nutrient-dense foods when appetite returns

Note: Specific dietary advice for medical conditions (diabetes, hypertension, kidney disease etc.) must come from a licensed dietitian or doctor.

=== WHEN TO MONITOR SYMPTOMS AT HOME VS. SEEK CARE ===

Monitor at Home (mild, improving symptoms in otherwise healthy adults):
- Mild cold or viral illness with improving trajectory
- Low-grade fever responding to supportive care and trending down
- Mild headache resolving with rest and hydration
- Mild muscle soreness after exertion
- Mild stomach upset improving with rest and bland diet

Seek Medical Care (consult a doctor within 1–2 days):
- Symptoms not improving after 3–5 days
- Fever recurring after initially resolving
- Symptoms mild but persistent and affecting daily function
- New symptoms appearing in addition to initial ones

Seek Immediate Emergency Care:
- Chest pain, breathing difficulty, signs of stroke (face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty)
- Severe abdominal pain
- Uncontrolled bleeding
- Severe allergic reaction (throat swelling, difficulty breathing)
- Loss of consciousness or seizure
- High fever with stiff neck, rash, or confusion
- Any symptom you feel is "not right" or rapidly worsening

REMEMBER: When in doubt — consult a licensed medical professional. Err on the side of seeking care.
